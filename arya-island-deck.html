The FORCE Framework
Five disciplines, embedded in one contract.

A contractual architecture built for transition, not just construction.

The FORCE Framework restates five variables — currently treated as aspirations layered on top of delivery — as contractual preconditions written in from award. What follows is not a new form of contract; it is the same instruments, rebuilt around the outcomes transition actually requires.

The premise
Five variables, one instrument. No aspiration is contractual until it is priced, triggered and enforceable.
Where FORCE is applied
Pace, incentive, governance, risk and stakeholder architecture — the five pillars in the sections that follow.
F

Fourth-class metrics

Pace, carbon and culture measured alongside cost and time.

O

Outcome-aligned incentives

Risk and reward tied to transition outcomes, not just completion.

R

Regulatory adaptability

Trigger-based milestones that flex with regulatory and technology change.

C

Culture readiness

Leadership, environment, architecture and resource as contractual preconditions.

E

Embedded governance

Oversight instruments written into the contract, not layered on afterwards.

Pace Alignment
Contracts that move at the speed of transition.

Milestones triggered by conditions, not calendars.

A trigger-based milestone architecture ties delivery gates to defined external conditions — regulatory publication, technology cost thresholds, demand signals — so pace adapts without renegotiation. This does not remove deadlines; it removes the single point of failure of a decade-long asset bound to one frozen date.

Incentive Architecture
Risk and reward shared, not transferred.

Gain-share instruments that align contractor and client outcomes.

FORCE replaces lump-sum pricing with a shared-risk, shared-gain mechanism: a target cost baseline with a pain and gain share formula tied to transition outcomes as well as cost and time. The commercial case for proposing a better delivery method now exists.

Governance Integration

Risk Transparency
Risk registers as public contractual instruments.

What used to be commercially confidential becomes structurally visible.

Stakeholder Architecture
Mapping power against interest before pace is set.

Not every stakeholder needs the same contractual voice.

Stakeholders with high power and high interest receive formal contractual standing: step-in rights, reporting lines, escalation triggers. Others receive lighter-touch consultation. This prevents capture by a narrow set of high-power actors, and consultation fatigue among the rest.

Legislative Roadmap
From framework to statute in three years.

FORCE requires legislation, not just contract templates.

Publication obligations need to override commercial confidentiality norms uniformly across the market, which requires statutory backing. The sequence is deliberate: an enabling statute establishes FORCE clauses and the Registry in law, the regulator's mandate is updated, and REP's 2027 pilot provides the first live test before wider rollout.

The Single Indicator
One ratio, plain enough to govern by.

TDER replaces cost and time as the only visible measure of success.

FORCE compresses five variables into one publishable indicator, the Transition Delivery Efficiency Ratio, reported at every milestone gate alongside cost and time. A ratio at or above 1.0 indicates the programme is delivering its contracted outcomes faster than its deviation is eroding value. TDER does not replace cost and time reporting — it sits alongside them.
